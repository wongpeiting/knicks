METHODOLOGY NOTES
=================

Data collection
- Automated scraper runs every ~45 minutes via GitHub Actions (cron).
- Each scrape captures a snapshot of StubHub's performer and event pages.
- Data is captured via Playwright (headless browser) with stealth measures to bypass AWS WAF.
- Prices shown are per-ticket, USD, including all fees (as displayed on StubHub).
- The listing JSON is intercepted from the raw HTTP response before React hydration removes it from the DOM.

What each data point represents
- A snapshot of what was listed on StubHub at that moment.
- NOT a continuous feed — there are gaps between scrapes.
- The scraper captures the top 40 "Recommended" listings per event, plus section-level min prices for all sections (~80-100 per event).

What you CAN claim
- "At [time], the cheapest listed ticket was $X"
- "Between [time] and [time], the floor price moved from $X to $Y"
- "The number of active listings dropped from X to Y over N hours"
- "The median section price was $X" (more stable than floor)
- "N listings disappeared in the hours before tipoff"

What you CANNOT claim
- "Prices fell steadily" — we don't know what happened between scrapes.
- "The lowest price was $X" — it's the lowest we OBSERVED; actual low could have been lower between scrapes.
- "X tickets were sold" — we can only say listings disappeared; sellers may have pulled unsold listings (especially at tipoff).

Known data gaps
- GitHub Actions cron scheduling is unreliable on the free tier. GitHub's own documentation states: "During periods of high loads of GitHub Actions workflow runs, the schedule event may be delayed." In practice, scheduled jobs are frequently skipped entirely, not just delayed.
- With a single hourly cron (0 * * * *), the scraper ran an average of once every 4.5 hours instead of every hour in the earlier phases of the project. Moving to off-peak minutes and multiple staggered slots improved reliability to an average gap of ~41 minutes, but gaps of 2-5 hours still occurred unpredictably.
- The scraper was manually triggered during critical periods (e.g. game day) to fill gaps.
- StubHub blocked GitHub Actions IPs from ~22:21 to ~00:49 UTC on Jun 5-6 (during and immediately after Game 2). This was caused by too many rapid requests (~8 scrapes in 2 hours) triggering StubHub's AWS WAF anti-bot protection. No data was captured in this ~2.5 hour window.
- Some scrapes returned non-Knicks events due to StubHub's dynamic content, resulting in occasional non-NBA data in the CSVs.
- Bottom line: this dataset has irregular sampling intervals. It should be treated as a series of snapshots, not a continuous time series. Any interpolation between points is speculative.

Floor price vs median
- "Floor price" = cheapest single listing. Volatile — one seller listing or removing a cheap ticket can swing it 10-20% between scrapes.
- "Median section price" = median of all sections' min prices. More stable and better represents the overall market.
- For reporting, prefer median or use floor with caveats about volatility.

Supply metrics
- "Total listings" = number of active seller listings. Reliable indicator of supply changes.
- "Ticket count" = total individual seats across all listings in a section.
- Listings disappearing does not equal tickets sold. Sellers can pull listings at any time. Pre-tipoff disappearances at low prices are likely sales; post-tipoff disappearances are likely pulled.

Individual listing tracking
- Each listing has a unique listing_id that persists while the seller's listing is active.
- We only capture the top 40 per event per scrape, so a listing may drop out of our view without being sold.
- Price changes on a single listing_id across scrapes = seller repricing, not a different ticket.

StubHub's reported metrics
- "X people viewed events in the past hour" — StubHub's own number, not independently verified.
- "Followers" — StubHub follower count, self-reported.
- Tags like "Hottest event", "Selling fast", "Best value" — StubHub's own demand signals, criteria unknown.

Recommended language for publication
- "StubHub data captured at approximately 45-minute intervals shows..."
- "The cheapest available ticket dropped from $773 to $480 in the 20 hours before tipoff, based on periodic snapshots of the marketplace"
- "At least 124 listings were removed from StubHub in the hours surrounding Game 2"
- Always note: "Prices are asking prices set by resellers on StubHub and may differ from face value"
